Q4. What is a literature review and when should I write it?
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  CORRECT ANSWER:
  The literature review is written after the approval of your topic and guidance form. It sets the premises and defines the knowledge base for your thesis. It must be approximately 10 pages long, concentrate on the key themes, concepts and knowledge base of your thesis, use correctly cited academic sources, have logical text organisation and headings, and follow the Karelia UAS thesis reporting instructions.   LLM ANSWER:
  A literature review is a comprehensive analysis of existing research on a specific topic or question. It's a critical evaluation of the relevant literature that provides an overview of what has been studied, discovered, and theorized about your research subject.
  
  **When to write a literature review:**
  
  You should write a literature review at various stages of your research project:
  
  1. **Before starting your research**: Conducting a thorough literature review helps you understand the existing body of knowledge on your topic, identify gaps in current research, and develop a clear research question or hypothesis.
  2. **During the research design phase**: A literature review can inform your methodology, sampling strategy, data collection methods, and analysis techniques.
  3. **As part of a thesis or dissertation proposal**: Including a literature review as part of your research proposal demonstrates that you have a thorough understanding of the topic and can help justify your proposed research.
  
  **What to include in a literature review:**
  
  A typical literature review should cover:
  
  1. **Background and context**: Provide an overview of the topic, its significance, and any relevant historical or theoretical frameworks.
  2. **Key studies and findings**: Discuss and analyze the most important and influential research on your topic, highlighting key discoveries, methodologies, and conclusions.
  3. **Methodological approaches**: Evaluate the methods used in previous studies, highlighting their strengths and limitations.
  4. **Gaps and areas for future research**: Identify areas where current research is lacking or where new contributions can be made.
  5. **Critical evaluation**: Critique existing research, identifying biases, flaws, or inconsistencies.
  
  **Types of literature reviews:**
  
  There are two main types:
  
  1. **Systematic review**: A comprehensive and structured synthesis of existing research on a specific topic, often using systematic methods to identify and include studies.
  2. **Nonsystematic review**: A more general overview of the literature, which may not follow a systematic approach.
  
  **Best practices for writing a literature review:**
  
  1. **Start with a clear question or research focus**.
  2. **Use a structured framework** (e.g., a table or matrix) to organize and analyze your findings.
  3. **Evaluate each study critically**, considering its methodology, sample size, results, and conclusions.
  4. **Synthesize the information**, identifying patterns, themes, and areas for future research.
  5. **Keep it concise**, focusing on key studies and findings that are most relevant to your research question.
  
  Remember that a literature review is not just a summary of existing research; it's an analysis and critique of that research, providing new insights and perspectives on your topic.

  LLM ANSWER:
  The length of a literature review can vary depending on the specific requirements of your project, course, or journal. However, here are some general guidelines:
  
  **Academic papers:**
  
  * Typically, a literature review for an academic paper should be around 10-20% of the total word count.
  * For a master's thesis, it might be 30-40 pages (around 7,500-10,000 words).
  * For a doctoral dissertation, it could be 50-100 pages (around 12,500-25,000 words) or more.
  
  **Undergraduate and graduate projects:**
  
  * For an undergraduate project, the literature review might be around 2-5 pages (around 500-1,250 words).
  * For a graduate project, such as a master's thesis proposal, it could be around 10-20 pages (around 2,500-5,000 words).
  
  **Key components of a literature review:**
  
  Regardless of the length, a good literature review should include:
  
  1. **Introduction**: Brief overview of the research topic, its significance, and the purpose of the literature review.
  2. **Background**: Historical context, major milestones, and influential studies related to the research topic.
  3. **Review of existing literature**: In-depth analysis of relevant studies, including their methodologies, findings, and implications.
  4. **Gaps in the current knowledge**: Identification of areas that need further investigation or debate.
  5. **Theoretical framework**: Discussion of theoretical perspectives, models, or frameworks that underpin your research question or topic.
  6. **Methodological debates**: Critique of existing methodologies and discussion of alternative approaches to your research question or topic.
  7. **Implications for practice**: Discussion of the practical implications of your findings, including potential applications and limitations.
  
  **Additional tips:**
  
  * Use headings and subheadings to organize your literature review and make it easier to read.
  * Provide a clear argument or thesis statement that ties together your discussion of existing research.
  * Use academic language and tone throughout your literature review.
  * Include relevant figures, tables, and diagrams to illustrate key concepts and findings.
  * Properly cite all sources in the required citation style.
  
  Remember, these are general guidelines. Be sure to consult with your instructor or advisor for specific requirements regarding the length and content of your literature review.

  10. **Limitations**: Acknowledgment of potential limitations or challenges that may impact the validity or generalizability of your findings.

  12. **Sources and references**: A list of sources used to support your literature review and research design.

  LLM ANSWER:
  Paraphrasing sources is an essential skill when writing a thesis, as it allows you to present others' ideas in your own words while maintaining the original meaning. Here are some tips on how to paraphrase sources effectively:
  
  1. **Read and understand the source**: Before attempting to paraphrase, make sure you comprehend the author's argument, methodology, and conclusions.
  2. **Identify key points**: Break down the text into its main ideas, supporting evidence, and analytical arguments. Focus on the most important concepts that need to be presented in your thesis.
  3. **Use your own words**: Avoid copying the original text verbatim or using similar sentence structures. Instead, rewrite the content in your own voice and style.
  4. **Maintain the original meaning**: Paraphrasing should not alter the author's intended message. Ensure that you accurately convey their ideas, even if you're expressing them differently.
  5. **Use synonyms and paraphrase phrases**: Replace key terms with their synonyms or rephrase sentences to create new expressions. This will help prevent plagiarism and ensure your writing is original.
  6. **Avoid close paraphrasing**: Refrain from rewriting the same sentence or phrase multiple times, as this can be seen as an attempt to disguise plagiarism.
  7. **Use transitional phrases**: When integrating a paraphrased idea into your text, use transitional words and phrases (e.g., "accordingly," "however," or "in addition") to guide the reader through the flow of your argument.
  
  Here are some examples of paraphrasing techniques:
  
  * Replace key terms with synonyms:
  	+ Original: "The researcher employed a mixed-methods approach."
  	Paraphrased: "A combination of qualitative and quantitative methods was used in this study."
  * Rephrase sentences to create new expressions:
  	+ Original: "This phenomenon has been extensively studied, but its causes remain unclear."
  	Paraphrased: "Despite numerous investigations, the underlying reasons for this phenomenon are still unknown."
  * Use your own words to summarize an idea:
  	+ Original: "The study's findings indicate that the treatment had a positive effect on patient outcomes."
  	Paraphrased: "The results showed that the treatment significantly improved patient outcomes."
  
  Best practices:
  
  1. **Keep track of sources**: Document each paraphrased source in your citation management system, just as you would for direct quotes.
